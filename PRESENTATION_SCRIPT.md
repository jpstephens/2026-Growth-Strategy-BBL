## SLIDE 3: You've Already Proven This Works
**Executive Thesis**

We've been asking Patrick to do two jobs that require completely different mindsets. That's not a Patrick problem. That's a leadership problem. We set up the wrong structure, and we're going to fix it."

**Key point to emphasize:**
- The constraint is structure, not people
- Leadership owns the past mistakes

## SLIDE 4: The Foundation: Proven Unit Economics
**Current State Metrics**

**Say this:**
"Let's look at our baseline numbers. 9 active customers. 71 loads per month. About $15,000 in monthly profit.

These are good numbers for where we are. The question is: how do we get from $15K to $100K? That's roughly a 7x increase. It's aggressive, but it's achievable if we execute the plan I'm about to show you."

**Numbers to point to:**
- 9 customers → need 65-70
- $15K profit → need $100K
- 7x growth required

---

## SLIDE 5: January 2026 Snapshot
**Customer Concentration**

**Say this:**
"Here's something we need to address head-on. Clearway and Frontier together represent 44% of our revenue. That's concentration risk.

I want to be clear: we're not going to fire these customers. They're great customers. But we need to dilute their percentage by adding more customers like them. Our goal is no single customer above 12% of revenue. That protects the business."

**Key point to emphasize:**
- Goal is diversification through growth, not dropping customers
- Target: <12% concentration per customer

# ACT 2: OWNERSHIP (Slides 6-9)
**Goal: Own past mistakes so Patrick and David don't feel blamed**

---

## SLIDE 6: How We Got Here
**Leadership Takes Responsibility**

**Say this:**
"Now let me be direct about what leadership got wrong. We set up a structure where Patrick was expected to hunt new business AND manage existing customers AND handle operations issues AND fight fires.

That was our mistake. Not Patrick's. We created an impossible situation and then wondered why growth stalled. That changes today."

**Pause here. This is important for buy-in.**

**Key points:**
- Leadership designed the broken structure
- Patrick was set up to fail
- No blame on execution - blame on design

---

## SLIDE 7: Two Jobs, One Person
**The Problem**

**Say this:**
"Here's what that looked like in practice. Patrick would start calling prospects at 9am. By 9:30, there's a delivery issue. By 10, a customer needs a quote. By 11, a carrier problem.

The result? Maybe 10 actual prospecting calls in a day instead of 40. Reactive mode. Constant firefighting. No time to build pipeline. Of course growth stalled - there was no protected time to make it happen."

**Key point to emphasize:**
- Interruptions killed sales activity
- Reactive mode is the enemy of growth

---

## SLIDE 8: Specialization Drives Productivity
**Why Sales & Ops Are Incompatible**

**Say this:**
"Here's why this matters so much. Sales and operations require completely different mindsets.

Sales is future-focused. You're thinking about next month's customers, building relationships, playing the long game. Operations is present-focused. You're solving today's problems, right now, immediately.

When you try to do both, operations always wins because it's urgent. Sales gets pushed because it feels less urgent - but it's actually more important for growth."

**Key insight:**
- Urgent (ops) always beats important (sales) unless you protect important
- One person cannot context-switch effectively between these mindsets

---

## SLIDE 9: The Growth Trajectory
**The Math of Divided Attention**

**Say this:**
"Let me show you the math of why this matters. Every hour Patrick spends on operations is an hour not spent building pipeline.

If Patrick makes 40 calls a day, that's roughly 8 conversations, which leads to 2 qualified prospects per day. Multiply that over a week and a month - that's how you get 4-5 new customers monthly.

But if he's only making 10 calls because of interruptions? The math doesn't work. You can't scale on 10 calls a day."

**Numbers to emphasize:**
- 40 calls = 8 conversations = 2 qualified = growth
- 10 calls = 2 conversations = maybe 1 qualified = stagnation

**Say this:**
"Patrick, here's your new role. One job: new customer acquisition. That's it.

Protected calling time is 8am to noon, every single day. No meetings. No ops issues. No interruptions. During those four hours, you're building pipeline. Period.

Your target is 200+ dials per week, which should yield 4-5 new customers per month based on our conversion rates."

**What Patrick owns:**
- New customer acquisition
- First load (books and monitors personally)
- Handoff to David after first success
- His own call schedule

**What Patrick does NOT do:**
- Day-to-day operations after handoff
- Carrier issues
- Customer service escalations
- Quotes for existing customers

---

## SLIDE 12: David's Role & Targets
**100% Operations Focus**

**Say this:**
"David, you own operations. Completely. Not 'help with operations' - own it.

After Patrick hands off a customer, you handle everything. Booking, tracking, communication, carrier relationships, problem-solving. It's your domain.

Your targets are 95%+ on-time delivery, issue resolution in under 2 hours, and building a carrier network we can rely on."

**What David owns:**
- All load execution after handoff
- Carrier relationships and development
- Day-to-day customer communication
- Operational process improvements

**What David does NOT do:**
- New customer acquisition
- Pricing decisions
- Sales calls or prospecting

## SLIDE 13: How One Customer Grows in Value
**Customer Ramp Model**

**Say this:**
"Here's something critical to understand. Customers don't arrive at full volume. They ramp up over time as trust builds.

Month 1, a new customer might give us 1-2 loads - they're testing us. By month 3, if we execute well, that's 8-12 loads. By month 6, we're at 15-25 loads per month.

This is why early acquisition matters so much. A customer acquired in January contributes meaningful volume by summer. A customer acquired in October barely contributes before year-end."

**Ramp timeline:**
- Month 1: 1-2 loads (trial)
- Month 2: 4-6 loads (building confidence)
- Month 3: 8-12 loads (regular shipper)
- Month 6+: 15-25 loads (established)

---

## SLIDE 14: Top Customer Profiles
**Customer Profitability**

**Say this:**
"A customer we acquire in January and retain all year generates approximately $38,000 in margin. That's one customer.

This is why David's role is so critical. Every customer Patrick brings in needs to be retained. Retention isn't just nice to have - it's the math that makes the whole model work.

If we acquire but don't retain, we're running on a treadmill. Acquire AND retain - that's how we scale."

**Key insight:**
- Acquisition without retention = treadmill
- David's execution directly impacts whether growth compounds

Third: Clear handoff. Patrick owns the customer through first successful load. Then David takes over completely. Clean transition, no ambiguity."

## SLIDE 17: Patrick's Protected Schedule
**8am-12pm Daily**

**Say this:**

## SLIDE 18: The Sales Funnel Math
**Conversion Formula**

**Say this:**
"Here's the math that makes this work. 200 dials per week. That's 40 per day during protected calling time.

Of those 200 dials, about 35 become actual conversations - that's a 17.5% connect rate, which is realistic for cold calling.

Of those 35 conversations, about 9 will be qualified prospects - they have freight, they're decision makers, they're potentially a fit.

Of those 9 qualified, about 4-5 will close within a reasonable timeframe. That's a 50% close rate on qualified opportunities.

So: 200 dials → 35 conversations → 9 qualified → 4-5 customers. Every week. That's the machine."

**The formula:**
- 40 dials/day × 5 days = 200 dials/week
- 17.5% connect rate = 35 conversations
- 25% qualify rate = 9 qualified
- 50% close rate = 4-5 customers

## SLIDE 20: Sales → Operations Handoff
**How Customers Transfer**

**Say this:**
"The handoff is critical. Here's exactly how it works.

Step 1: Patrick books the first load personally. He made the promise, he delivers on it.

Step 2: Patrick monitors that first load closely. If anything goes wrong, he's on it.

Step 3: After successful first delivery, Patrick sends an intro email. 'David is your day-to-day contact going forward. He'll take great care of you.'

Step 4: David takes over everything - all future booking, tracking, communication.

Step 5: Patrick stays available only for relationship escalations. The kind where a customer says 'I want to talk to Patrick.' That's it.

Clean handoff. No ambiguity. Customer knows who to call."

## SLIDE 28: David's Priority Framework
**How to Decide What to Work On**

**Say this:**
"David, this is your decision framework for prioritizing work.

Priority 1 - Red: Active load issues. If a load is in trouble, drop everything. Handle it immediately.

Priority 2 - Yellow: Upcoming pickups. Confirm carriers, send BOLs, check appointments. Handle before end of day.

Priority 3 - Green: Admin and carrier development. Invoicing, carrier vetting, building relationships. Do this when Priority 1 and 2 are clear.

The rule is simple: Is a load in trouble? Handle it. If not, check tomorrow's pickups. If those are good, build the carrier network."

Proven carriers: 5+ loads, 90%+ on-time, good communication. Regular consideration.

Trial carriers: New, still evaluating. Start them on lower-risk loads.

The goal by year-end: 50+ carriers total, at least 10 in the Preferred tier. Direct carrier relationships mean better margins and more reliable service."
